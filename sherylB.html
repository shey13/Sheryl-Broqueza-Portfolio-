SKU-Led vs Editorial Banner Creative

The Problem

ZALORA's onsite banners relied heavily on editorial/lifestyle imagery — aspirational but abstract. Users were scanning past banners without engaging because the creative didn't show them what they could actually buy. CTR on campaign banners was underperforming, and the connection between banner impression and purchase was weak.

The Hypothesis

Showing actual product SKUs on banners (real items at real prices) would outperform editorial imagery because it reduces cognitive load — users don't need to imagine what's inside the campaign. The product is the message. Based on behavioral research showing users scan banners in 2.6 seconds, the hypothesis was that SKU imagery would provide faster comprehension and stronger purchase intent signals.

Test Design

  • Control: Editorial/lifestyle banner imagery (standard creative)
  • Variation: SKU-led banners featuring actual products with prices
  • Segments: Women and Men tested separately
  • Platform: Android & iOS
  • Duration: Initial 1-day flash test → validated with 4-day extended run

Iteration Timeline

May 2022
Initial 1-day test — Women segment. Massive CTR and NMV uplift confirmed.
+435% CTR
May 2022
Same-day test — Men segment. Strong uplift across all metrics.
+206% CTR
Feb 2023
Extended 4-day rerun — Women. CTR held (+47%) but CR dropped due to high click volume diluting conversion. Key learning: high CTR ≠ high CR at scale.
+47% CTR, CR↓
Feb 2023
Extended 4-day rerun — Men. CTR still positive (+23%), same CR pattern as Women.
+23% CTR, CR↓

The Learning

SKU-led creative is dramatically more effective at driving engagement — but at high click volumes, CR can decline as more "browsers" enter the funnel. The solution wasn't to revert to editorial, but to use SKU banners for flash sales (high intent) and editorial for always-on (brand building). This learning was codified into ZALORA's creative playbook across markets.

Business Impact

SKU-led creative is now the default for tactical and flash sale campaigns at ZALORA Philippines. The +148% NMV uplift from the initial Women's test alone demonstrated the revenue ceiling of the old editorial-only approach. The 4-day rerun's "failure" was equally valuable — it informed a context-dependent creative strategy rather than a blanket rule.

The Problem

ZALORA's standard homepage recommendation feed delivered a 26.98% CTR and 28.29% add-to-cart — decent, but plateauing. More critically, a segment of users with zero or very low product detail views (PDV) were bouncing without discovering products that matched their preferences. The traditional homepage structure wasn't converting low-engagement traffic into buyers.

The Hypothesis

A dedicated "Daily Style Feed" — a curated, campaign-aligned recommendation page with full content flexibility — would outperform the standard RecFeed by offering personalized exposure at scale. By routing low/zero-PDV users specifically into this feed, we could convert previously disengaged traffic. Unlike the traditional homepage, this page would have no category or homepage content logic constraints — pure product-first discovery.

Solution Architecture

  • Discovery engine positioned on the app home screen with personalized product exposure and agile brand/campaign merchandising
  • Full content flexibility — unlike traditional category or homepage limitations, the feed acts as a conversion amplifier by increasing product interaction volume per session
  • Low-PDV user routing — dedicated onsite pages created for users with zero or low product views, directing them into the Daily Style Feed
  • Social amplification — SM channels used to augment awareness and drive traffic into the feed
  • Rolled out live across all ZALORA markets

The Results

The Daily Style Feed nearly doubled CTR compared to the standard RecFeed (52% vs 26.98%) with a higher add-to-cart rate (31.7% vs 28.29%). The full funnel held strong: 48.5% product click-through → 98.5% proceeding to product view → 30.9% PDP-to-cart → 5.5% attributed purchase from product adders. Now live across all ZALORA markets.

Business Impact

This wasn't a one-off test — it's a permanent product now live on every ZALORA market's app home screen. It solved two problems at once: improving discovery for engaged users and creating a targeted intervention for low/zero-PDV users who would otherwise churn. The nearly 2x CTR uplift represents a structural improvement to ZALORA's product discovery infrastructure, not just a campaign win.

The Problem

ZALORA's voucher banners traditionally displayed the promo code directly on the banner (e.g., "use code: 43RED"). The assumption was that showing the code upfront would make redemption easier. But was this actually helping conversion, or was it adding visual noise and reducing the click incentive?

The Hypothesis

Removing the visible voucher code and replacing it with a clear CTA button ("Shop Now") would increase CTR and conversion by creating a stronger action signal. The code could be auto-applied at checkout or shown post-click. This trades transparency for urgency — the CTA creates forward momentum that a static code display doesn't.

Iteration Timeline

Apr 17, 2023
Flash sale (1–7 PM). CTA banner vs Code banner. Women/Men/Kids.
+14.53% CTR, +9% CR, +14% NMV
Apr 20, 2023
Flash sale v2 (2–8 PM). Same structure, different time window. Confirmed directional results.
+6.98% CTR, +6% CR, +48% NMV
May 2–4, 2023
Extended baseline (1 week). Validated that the effect held beyond flash sale urgency.
+4.69% CTR, +2% CR, +48% NMV

The Learning

The "Without Code + CTA" treatment won across all three iterations — flash, repeat flash, and extended baseline — confirming this wasn't a novelty effect. The magnitude was highest in flash sales (urgency amplifies CTA power) but still significant in always-on contexts. This approach is now standard for ZALORA's voucher campaigns.

Why This Matters

This case demonstrates iterated validation — not trusting a single test, but running three progressively different environments (short flash, different flash, extended baseline) to confirm the learning is robust. It also shows how a small UX change (hiding the code, adding a CTA) can unlock double-digit revenue uplift at zero implementation cost.

The Problem

ZALORA's App Grid content (segmented promotional copy across 6 audience segments) was updated manually every week — 30 minutes per segment, 3 hours per cycle. The process was error-prone, created copy inconsistencies across segments, and caused timing risks for critical campaigns. With plans to scale to 10+ segments, the manual approach was becoming a bottleneck.

The Solution

Built an n8n automation workflow that ingests approved copy from a source sheet, validates segment mapping and field formatting, transforms to the App Grid API schema, then performs batch updates with retry logic, logging, Slack notifications, and optional rollback. Separately, built a trend intelligence pipeline (n8n + GPT + TikTok Creative Center + Pinterest Trends) to generate structured monthly campaign briefs — also CEO-approved.

Business Case Breakdown

  • One-time build cost: €920 (10 mandays)
  • Annual labour savings: €936 (156 hours/year of manual work eliminated)
  • Campaign uplift protected: €10,000/year (5 critical campaigns × €200K NMV × 1% timing-dependent uplift)
  • Error reduction: €216/year (copy error rework eliminated)
  • Scalability unlocked: €624/year (4 new segments enabled without headcount growth)
  • Total annualized benefit: €11,848 → Net first-year return of €10,928

The Learning

The biggest win wasn't the labour savings — it was protecting campaign timing. For 5 key campaigns each worth €200K NMV, ensuring on-time copy deployment protected ~1% uplift per campaign. This reframing (from "saving 3 hours/week" to "protecting €10K/year in revenue") was what got CEO approval. Always tie automation to revenue impact, not just efficiency.

Why This Matters

This case shows the ability to think commercially about technical solutions — building the ROI model, identifying the revenue protection angle, presenting to CEO-level stakeholders, and then actually building and shipping the technical solution. It also demonstrates scalability thinking: the workflow decouples growth from headcount.

Methodology
How I Work

Every experiment follows the same discipline — regardless of whether it's a 3-hour flash test or a multi-market product launch.

01

Observe & Hypothesize

Start with data, not opinions. Identify underperforming metrics, segment the problem, and form a testable hypothesis grounded in user behavior research. Every test has a clear "because we believe [X] will cause [Y]" statement before it runs.

02

Test with Discipline

Controlled experiments with clean segmentation — platform (iOS/Android), audience (Women/Men/Kids), and duration (flash vs extended) are always defined upfront. I don't trust single tests; I iterate across conditions to validate that results are robust, not novelty effects.

03

Scale & Codify

Winning patterns become playbooks. SKU-led creative is now standard for tactical campaigns. CTA-over-code is the default for vouchers. Learnings are documented and shared across markets so a single test in PH can benefit SG, ID, MY, and HK without re-running.
